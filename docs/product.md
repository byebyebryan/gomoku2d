# Product

## What we're building

A browser-based Gomoku game where you can play against bots, play against other
people online, work through puzzles, and go back through your games to see
where they turned. The Rust bot lab behind it isn't a side project — it's the
engine that makes the interesting features possible.

This document describes the broader target product and its long-term pillars.
The current local-first `v0.2` slice and rollout order live in `app_design.md` and
`roadmap.md`.

## Pillars

### 1. Lab-as-feature-source

The bot lab is a product input, not scaffolding. Features that make this game
different from "yet another 5-in-a-row" come from being able to run the Rust
core and bots at arbitrary positions:

- **Puzzles** generated by searching positions from real games for
  forced-win branches, then checking a human can find them.
- **Replay analysis** that re-runs each move through a stronger bot to
  tag "critical moments" — where the evaluation swung, what the better move
  was, what the opponent's best reply would have been.
- **"Can you save this game?"** mode that drops you into a losing position
  from one of your games and asks you to fight out of it against the bot
  that would have punished you.

Running the same Rust core on the server also lets trusted online matches be
re-verified against the authoritative rules engine, but that is a trust
boundary (see `backend.md`), not a lab-powered feature in its own right.

If a feature idea doesn't benefit from the lab, that's fine; but the features
we're most excited about all do.

### 2. Guest-first on-ramp, cloud-backed depth

The first session should feel disposable and frictionless; the deeper features
should feel durable once you choose to sign in. Specifically:

- First meaningful interaction creates a **local guest profile** on the device.
  You can play bot matches and build up local history without creating a cloud
  account.
- Signing in with Google or GitHub **promotes** the local guest state to a
  cloud-backed profile without making the user feel they "started over."
- **Local guest data** is the source of truth for guest sessions. **Cloud
  state** becomes the source of truth for signed-in features such as synced
  history, shareable replays, online play, and anything public.
- Online human matches and any replay we intend to trust or share go through
  backend-managed flows rather than relying on client-only state.

### 3. Hybrid pixel + modern shell

The board keeps the pixel-art feel (we already have the assets, and it gives
the game a distinct look). Everything around the board — menus, lobby,
profile, replay controls — is a clean, modern DOM UI. A shared color palette
keeps the two halves cohesive rather than feeling like two games glued
together.

This isn't "canvas in a web page." It's a proper SPA where the canvas is one
component among many.

## Non-goals

Calling these out so scope stays honest:

- **Not ranked esports.** We'll track rating for matchmaking but we're not
  building leagues, tournaments, or anti-smurf systems.
- **Not a social network.** Friends lists and direct challenges are plausible;
  feeds, followers, and chat timelines are not.
- **Not native mobile.** Mobile-web should work. Native apps are out.
- **Not maximal polish.** This is a personal project. "Good enough to enjoy
  playing and show friends" beats "ship-grade AAA." If a feature takes six
  months to polish, cut it.

## Success looks like

- A stranger can open the URL, play a bot match as a guest, decide they care,
  sign in with Google, keep their progress, and review or share a saved replay
  without reading instructions.
- At least one feature in the game exists specifically *because* we have a
  bot lab behind it (puzzles, critical-move tagging, or save-this-game).
- The pieces stay legible: someone reading the repo can tell what the game
  is, what the lab is, and how they connect, without a tour.
